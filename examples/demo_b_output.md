## Stage 2 — Plain Language Retelling

If you run a one-person business and you end most days feeling depleted in a way that doesn't quite match how hard your actual work was, this paper has a diagnosis for that.

The researchers looked at 47 independent knowledge workers — consultants, researchers, writers, analysts — and compared them to 43 salaried workers with equivalent experience and domain expertise. They tracked how often people switched tasks and tested their executive function (the cognitive capacity for focus, inhibition, and decision-making) at the beginning and end of each workday over ten days.

The first finding is simply quantitative: independent workers switched tasks about twice as often as salaried employees — 6.7 times per hour versus 3.2. But the more important finding is what those switches were. Most of them were not creative task-to-task transitions. They were administrative interruptions: responding to an inquiry, sending an invoice, scheduling something. The overhead of running the business was eating into the capacity to do the work of the business.

The second finding is the mechanistic link. The researchers found that by end of day, independent workers' executive function had declined roughly twice as much as their salaried peers' — an 18% drop versus 9% on the test measuring inhibition, the capacity to stay on task and filter out competing demands. The interpretation the authors offer is that self-managing the portfolio — deciding what to do next, triaging what just arrived, handling what's urgent — draws from the same pool of cognitive resources as primary knowledge work. You spend down the same battery doing meta-decisions as you do doing the actual work.

A subset of independent workers with externalized priority systems — a written plan reviewed before starting work, or a rule committing to a single primary task per half-day block — showed end-of-day executive function decline indistinguishable from salaried controls. Not less decline than average independents: as little decline as people working inside organizations with managers and scheduled meetings.

The finding that experience does not attenuate the effect is perhaps the most practically significant result. Veterans and newcomers showed similar levels of attention tax. The operators who had "figured it out" had done so by building systems that handled the sequencing and triage decisions for them — not by becoming more cognitively resilient. The tax is structural. It is always owed. The only variable is whether you've designed a system that pays it from an administrative budget rather than your primary work capacity.

The authors' conclusion reframes the standard productivity diagnosis: the bottleneck for one-person knowledge businesses is almost never domain expertise. It is executive resource depletion from the overhead of self-management. And the evidence-supported intervention is system design, not habit formation.

## Stage 3 — Content Pack (Paper Mode)

### Research Brief

This paper introduces the concept of the "attention tax" — the cognitive overhead cost that one-person knowledge businesses pay simply by operating without organizational scaffolding. The study compared 47 independent knowledge workers to 43 salaried peers over ten days and found that independent workers switched tasks twice as often (6.7 vs. 3.2 times per hour), with most of those switches being administrative rather than core work. End-of-day executive function declined roughly twice as steeply for independent workers (18% vs. 9% on inhibition measures).

The key finding for solo operators: independent workers who used externalized priority systems — a written plan, a single-task commitment per half-day — showed end-of-day cognitive decline equivalent to employees in structured organizations. And the tax does not diminish with experience; it is structural.

For one-person businesses, this reframes the question. The productivity bottleneck is not expertise or discipline. It is self-management overhead consuming the same executive resources needed for primary knowledge work. The solution is not trying harder — it is designing systems that offload sequencing and triage decisions so that executive capacity remains available for the work itself.

### Business and Creator Angles

1. **Audit your task-switch pattern for three days.** Log every switch and categorize it as core work or administrative overhead. If admin switches exceed core-work switches before noon, you have a system design problem, not a focus problem.

2. **Test a single-primary-task commitment.** Based on Finding 3, commit to one defined primary task per half-day block for two weeks — no context-switching into that slot. Track whether end-of-day capacity (measured by how much creative work you can do after 4pm) changes.

3. **This paper is citable credibility for the "build systems, not habits" argument** — if you coach solo operators or write about independent work, "The Attention Tax" gives you a research frame and specific numbers to work with rather than purely anecdotal framing.

4. **The finding that experience does not attenuate the tax is a content angle on its own**: most advice to struggling solo operators implies it gets easier over time. This paper says it doesn't — unless you build structural solutions. That framing could anchor a newsletter issue or talk.

5. **Design your own "externalized priority system."** The paper identifies two approaches that worked: reviewing a written daily plan before starting work, and a single-task commitment rule. Experiment with one of these for one month and document what changes.
